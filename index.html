Follow Christopher Pollon on The Copper Road

Forget gold. Copper is worth much more to British Columbia as an export. The red metal ends up in smart phones, in cars, in plumbing and house wiring—as well as in landfills. How does it get there? Tyee Contributing Editor Christopher Pollon wanted to find out. So he set out on the Copper Road—from B.C. to Asia and back. Follow his journey.

Chapter One: The Mine

Christopher Pollon began his investigation just a few kilometers south of Princeton, B.C.. Six billion dollars worth of ore have been dug from the rightly named Copper Mountain area over the last century. Today, trucks the size of bungalows haul rock to be crushed for its red metal content.

Chapter Two: The Money

The next stop on Christopher Pollon's road was Vancouver, a motherlode of mine financing. With Toronto, the city capitalizes three-fifths of the entire world's mines. Chapter Three: The Smelter

Pursuing B.C.’s copper Christopher Pollon crossed the Pacific. Landing at Osaka, Japan, he grabbed a bullet train and then a ferry to Naoshima Island, where furnaces have been melting ore down into pure metal for almost a century. Only one of Japan’s own metal mines is still active, at Hishikari.

Chapter Four: The Factory

It was a short hop for Christopher Pollon, and the metal he’s following, from Japan across the East China Sea to the factory city of Kunshan, China. There B.C. copper finds its way into components used to make popular electronics. The Shanghai suburb is trying to hold off competition from up-and-coming centres inland: Yunnan, Chengdu and Chonqing.

Chapter Five: The Scrapyard

Back on this side of the Pacific, Christopher Pollon tracked what becomes of manufactured copper when its useful life is over. In B.C.'s Peace country, gas fields are generating plenty of scrap metal. Consolidated at A.B.C. Recycling, used copper competes head-to-head with virgin ore from mines like Alaska’s planned giant Pebble Mine.

Chapter Six: The Recycler

Back in the Lower Mainland, urbanites go through their cell phones in about a year; laptops in two or three. Christopher Pollon followed the stream of turned-in-for-recycling electronics to suburban Langley, where cell phones are collected for recycling at Sims Canada. Some of the copper ends up back at Naoshima island.
