Chapter 1: The Mine

Six billion dollars worth of copper have been dug from the rightly named Copper Mountain area, just south of Princeton, B.C. in the last century. After a dormant period, rising prices spurred by Asian demand brought the mine back to life. But now who's in charge?

Chapter 2: The Money

Vancouver is the motherlode of mine financing. With Toronto, the city’s financial sector capitalizes three-fifths of the world's mines. It's here that the cash needed to fund B.C.’s next generation of big copper strikes – like Galore Creek, Kerr-Sulphrets-Mitchell, and Schaft Creek – may eventually come. From here Pollon explores BC’s low-grade, high-risk copper casino...

Chapter 3: The Smelter

Raw copper from Copper Mountain is melted and refined at Japan's Naoshima Island. From the deck of a private ferry racing across the Seto inland sea from Osaka, the island appears as a tower of yellow rock on the horizon, tipped in a lush emerald. If this were a James Bond movie, Naoshima would be the island lair of an arch-villain.

Chapter 4: The Factory

It was a short hop for Christopher Pollon, and the metal he’s following, from Japan across the East China Sea to the factory city of Kunshan, China. There B.C. copper finds its way into components used to make popular electronics. The Shanghai suburb is trying to hold off competition from up-and-coming centres inland: Yunnan, Chengdu and Chonqing.

Chapter 5: The Scrapyard

Back on this side of the Pacific, Christopher Pollon tracked what becomes of manufactured copper when its useful life is over. In B.C.'s Peace country, gas fields are generating plenty of scrap metal. Consolidated at A.B.C. Recycling, used copper competes head-to-head with virgin ore from mines like Alaska’s planned giant Pebble Mine.

Chapter 6: The Recycler

Back in the Lower Mainland, urbanites go through their cell phones in about a year; laptops in two or three. Christopher Pollon followed the stream of turned-in-for-recycling electronics to suburban Langley, where cell phones are collected for recycling at Sims Canada. Some of the copper ends up back at Naoshima island.

Follow Christopher Pollon on The Copper Road

Forget gold. Copper is British Columbia's most lucrative mineral export. The red metal ends up in smart phones, in cars, in plumbing and house wiring—as well as in landfills. How does it get there? Tyee Contributing Editor Christopher Pollon wanted to find out. So he set out on the Copper Road—from B.C. to Asia and back.
